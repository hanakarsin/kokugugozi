Coronavirus update in Kenya: Scary statistics as 23 more patients test positive

 Posted on June 18, 2024  |  3 minutes  |  544 words  |  Enda Jantzen

- Kenya tested 2,100 more samples in the last 24 hours out of which 23 were confirmed to be positive for coronavirus

- The new infections spread across five counties with Nairobi and Mombasa registering 11 and five patients respectively

- At least 20 out of 47 counties reported positive cases of coronavirus even as some government health restrictions approached deadlines

Kenya has recorded an additional 23 coronavirus patients as the country enters the second month since the first case was reported on March 13, 2020

Health Chief Administrative Secretary (CAS) Rashid Aman said the new infections were registered from the 2,100 samples tested in the last 24 hours.

The 23 patients were spread in five counties with 11 being reported from Nairobi, five from Mombasa while Kajiado, Kiambu and Wajir counties registered two new cases each.

So far, 20 counties out of 47 have reported at least one case with Bomet being the latest.

With the rising cases, Kenya's confirmed cases worryingly moved to 781 just 19 shy of 800 while the casualties increased to 45 after three more lost the COVID-19 battle.

The worrying statistics on coronavirus spread in Kenya means the government will evaluate and review some of the measures it had put in place.

The health measures included cessation of movement in select counties, a national curfew and partial lockdown in some Mombasa and Nairobi estates.

More troubling was the fact that the dusk-to-dawn curfew which President Uhuru Kenyatta had extended for additional 21 days ends on Saturday, May 16.

During the Friday, May 15, press briefing, Aman told journalists the government would give its clear position on whether or not it should relax some of the restrictions.

On Thursday, May 14, Health CAS Mercy Mwangangi said Kenya just like the rest of the world was assessing its post-coronavirus strategies on resumption of normal operations.

"According to our scientists, this virus might be with us for a long time. We must continue with our new normal. The government is working on measures to ensure that we can go back to business in our new normal situation," she said.

The World Health Organisation Executive Director Mike Ryan warned the virus may not go away soon and could become another epidemic like any other.

"This virus just may become another endemic virus in our communities and this virus may never go away. HIV hasn't gone away," Ryan said. "I'm not comparing the two diseases but I think it is important that we're realistic. I don't think anyone can predict when or if this disease will disappear," he said during a media briefing in Geneva May 14 .
